Half of What Social Science Knows May Be Wrong

The largest audit of its kind tested hundreds of published findings in the behavioural and social sciences. The results should unsettle anyone who trusts the institutions of knowledge.

Published 1 April 2026

Credit: Ibrahim Rayintakath

Imagine a study lands on your desk. It's been published in a respected journal. The statistics look clean. The conclusions are clear. You want to build on it, fund it, perhaps let it shape a policy decision.

Now flip a coin.

That, researchers say, is roughly the chance that the finding will survive an independent retest.

A sweeping new collection of investigations, published this week in Nature and directly relevant to the fields WWTF funds, represents the most systematic audit ever conducted of the social and behavioural sciences. Four coordinated studies probed the field's most fundamental promises: that findings can be reproduced, that conclusions hold up under different methods, and that results replicate when someone else runs the experiment from scratch.

The audit did not set out to indict science. But the picture it assembled is difficult to look away from.

Across hundreds of papers and thousands of claims, the same patterns emerged again and again: data goes unshared, analyses diverge in the hands of different researchers, and roughly half of published results evaporate when someone tries to reproduce them. Yet buried inside the same evidence is a counterargument — proof that when researchers commit to transparency, the science holds.

The replication crisis, it turns out, has a cure. The field just hasn't decided to take it.

The three tests science keeps failing

Every published claim in the social sciences must answer three questions. They sound simple. They aren't.

Test 1: Reproducibility

Give someone your data. Can they get your answer?

Math doesn't lie. Two and two should make four regardless of who does the arithmetic. So when investigators obtained the raw data from 600 published papers — the spreadsheets, the code, the files behind the conclusions — they expected the results to follow.

They didn't. Not reliably.

Only about half produced findings that could be reproduced exactly. The culprit, more often than fraud or faulty logic, was silence: most researchers had never shared their materials at all. Untested by design.

~50% of results held up when investigators ran the original data themselves
Test 2: Analytical Robustness

Hand the same data to different scientists. Do they agree?

Now imagine a different kind of test. Same dataset. Different hands.

Researchers gave 100 published studies to multiple independent analyst teams, each free to make their own methodological calls — which variables to control for, which observations to exclude, how to handle outliers. Every choice was defensible. None were identical.

The conclusions diverged. Sometimes sharply. "The same data can tell different stories," one scientist involved in the project said, "depending on how you look at it." In a field where no single analysis is mandatory, that variability rarely gets disclosed — or even noticed.

100 studies reanalysed — analyst teams often reached different conclusions from identical data
Test 3: Replicability

Run the whole experiment again. Does it still work?

This is the hardest test — and the one that matters most. Not: can you recalculate someone's answer? But: if you go out and collect new data, independently, does the finding hold?

When researchers applied this test to 274 published claims, roughly half survived. The other half didn't.

Even the studies that made it through came back smaller. Effects that had seemed large and convincing in the original papers shrank — sometimes dramatically — when subjected to a second look. First discoveries, the data suggest, are frequently more exciting than they are true.

274 claims retested — about half replicated, most with weaker effects than first reported
The evidence for a fix

One corner of science kept passing. Here's what it did differently.

Not everything looked bleak. When investigators turned to economics and political science — fields that have long required researchers to publish their data and code — the results were strikingly different.

More than 85 percent of claims held up under computational review. Nearly three-quarters remained statistically significant when different analysts stress-tested them. The science was messier than it appeared, yes — about one in four papers contained a non-trivial coding error — but it was auditable. And what can be audited can be fixed.

Transparency is not just a scientific virtue. It turns out to be a measurable one.

85% of claims held up in fields where sharing data is the norm, not the exception

Many results in the social sciences don't stand up to immediate scrutiny. But that's the beginning of a conversation, not the end.

Brian Nosek — Psychologist & reproducibility researcher

The Studies

Four papers. One coordinated reckoning with what science can — and cannot — claim to know.

  1. 01

    Investigating the reproducibility of the social and behavioural sciences

    Researchers requested the underlying data and code from 600 published papers across 62 journals. Only about half produced results that held up exactly — and in many cases, the materials simply weren't available to test. The study is the clearest evidence yet that transparency, not intention, is what separates reproducible science from science taken on faith.

    ~50% exactly reproducible · 600 papers · 62 journals
  2. 02

    Investigating the analytical robustness of the social and behavioural sciences

    Dozens of analyst teams were given the same 100 datasets and asked to reach their own conclusions independently. What emerged was a field-wide Rorschach test: the same evidence, interpreted through different but defensible methodological lenses, frequently pointed in different directions. The implication is uncomfortable — published findings often reflect not just what the data shows, but who was looking.

    100 studies · Multiple analyst teams · Conclusions shift with analytical choices
  3. 03

    Investigating the replicability of the social and behavioral sciences

    An independent team of researchers went back to 274 published claims and ran each experiment again from the beginning — new participants, new data collection, no shortcuts. Roughly half held up. The other half didn't. And even among the survivors, the reported effects were almost always smaller than originally claimed, sometimes by a wide margin. Science, the study implies, has a systematic tendency to overstate its own discoveries.

    274 claims tested · ~50% replicated · Effect sizes smaller
  4. 04

    Reproducibility and robustness of economics and political science research

    Here is the counterargument, made in data. In economics and political science — disciplines with longstanding norms around data and code disclosure — more than 85 percent of papers held up under computational scrutiny, and nearly three-quarters remained statistically robust when different analysts stress-tested them. There were still problems: roughly one in four papers contained a non-trivial coding error. But the errors could be found, because the work could be checked. That, the authors argue, is the difference between a science that can correct itself and one that cannot.

    85%+ reproducible · 72% results stable · 25% had coding errors

The replication crisis didn't kill science. It may have saved it.

By turning the tools of empirical inquiry back on itself, the scientific community is doing something rare in any institution: demanding proof of its own proof. The path forward is not comfortable. But it is clear.
